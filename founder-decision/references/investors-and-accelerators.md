# Investor & Accelerator Wisdom

Distilled operating advice from the people who fund and coach thousands of startups —
Y Combinator / Startup School, Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), Sequoia, Founders Fund, Draper,
First Round, and the founders in their orbit. Apply the *idea* to the decision at hand.

## The one caveat that governs everything here

**Most of this wisdom optimizes for venture-scale, winner-take-most outcomes.** That's the
game VCs play — they need a few companies to return the whole fund, so their advice pushes
toward hypergrowth, big markets, and swinging for the fences. If the founder is building a
**bootstrapped, profitable, or lifestyle business**, applying blitzscale/VC logic blindly is
actively harmful — it can push them to burn cash and chase a scale they don't need or want.

So before applying anything below, ask: *is this founder actually playing the venture game?*
If yes, this is gold. If no, weight it accordingly (and reach for the bootstrapped
counter-canon — Rework, The $100 Startup — in `book-principles.md`).

## Y Combinator / Startup School
The most concentrated body of early-stage advice that exists. Core doctrines:
- **Make something people want.** The entire game. Everything else is secondary.
- **Build something a small number of people *love*, not something a lot of people *like*.**
  Intense love from a few beats mild interest from many — it's the seed of real growth.
- **Talk to your users. Do things that don't scale.** (Paul Graham — also in book-principles.)
- **Launch now.** A mediocre launch you learn from beats a perfect launch that never happens.
  You cannot learn what the market wants without exposing something to it.
- **Growth rate is the measure of a startup** (Graham). Pick one metric — usually weekly
  growth — and orient around it. If it's not growing, nothing else matters yet.
- **Default alive vs. default dead** (Graham). Know, at your current growth and burn, whether
  you reach profitability before the money runs out. Founders who don't know are usually dead.
- **Avoid "fake work."** Fundraising, PR, conferences, and endless meetings *feel*
  productive but aren't building or talking to users. Real work is product + customers.
- **The market is the most important factor** (Dalton Caldwell). A great team in a bad market
  loses. Don't be a brilliant solution hunting for a problem.
- **Co-founder disputes and equity** — split roughly equally among true co-founders; unequal
  splits and unclear roles are a top early killer (this echoes the "wrong team" failure mode).

**Apply to:** almost any early-stage decision — traction, focus, launch timing, what to work
on this week, whether you're actually alive.

## Andreessen Horowitz (a16z)
- **"The only thing that matters is getting to product/market fit"** (Marc Andreessen). Before
  PMF, obsess over nothing else. After PMF, the game changes to scaling.
- **Market matters most — "the market pulls the product out of the startup."** In a great
  market with real demand, the market drags the product to success. "Markets that don't
  exist don't care how smart you are."
- **Rank order for early startups: market > team > product.** A great product in a nonexistent
  market still fails; a mediocre product in a ravenous market gets pulled forward.
- **Software is eating the world** — look for where software can restructure an old industry.
- **Only two ways to compete: be cheaper or be better (differentiated).** "Better" is usually
  the more durable bet (ties to Zero to One).
- Horowitz's operating playbook (wartime CEO, hard calls) is in `book-principles.md`.

**Apply to:** market choice, "do we have PMF yet?", what to optimize before vs. after fit,
competitive positioning.

## Sequoia
- **"Why now?"** Every great startup has a timing thesis — some recent change (tech,
  regulation, behavior) that makes *this* the moment. If you can't answer why now, be worried.
- **Market size / TAM.** Sequoia funds big markets. Ask honestly how large this can get.
- **The prepared mind.** Clarity of thought and a crisp, simple articulation of the business
  signals a founder who understands their own venture.

**Apply to:** timing decisions, market-size honesty, whether the opportunity is big enough to
justify the sacrifice, sharpening your own thesis.

## Founders Fund (Peter Thiel)
- **Back the contrarian and ambitious.** "We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140
  characters" — a critique of incrementalism. Prefer bold, hard, differentiated bets over
  safe copies (this is Zero to One in VC form).
- **Definite optimism.** Have a specific plan for a better future, not a vague hope. Founders
  with a concrete, ambitious vision beat those hedging their bets.

**Apply to:** ambition/scope, whether an idea is bold enough, avoiding me-too positioning.

## Draper (Tim Draper / DFJ)
- **Viral / network-effect growth as an early bet.** Draper coined "viral marketing" (the
  Hotmail footer). Ask whether the product has a built-in reason for users to spread it.
- **Bet on grit and vision.** Draper backs founders with relentless drive and a big, even
  crazy, vision — and preaches high risk tolerance and contrarian conviction (early Bitcoin,
  Tesla, SpaceX, Skype).
- **The hero's journey.** Expect the venture to go through trials; persistence through the
  low point is the pattern, not a warning sign.

**Apply to:** growth/distribution decisions, risk appetite, contrarian bets, endurance.

## First Round & operator wisdom
- **Founder-led sales first.** The founder should sell to and learn from the first ~10–100
  customers personally before hiring salespeople — you can't outsource finding PMF.
- **Hire slow, fire fast.** A wrong hire (especially early) is expensive and contagious; a
  needed firing delayed compounds. (Echoes the "wrong team" and "avoiding the hard
  conversation" failure modes.)
- **Your first 10 customers teach you the business.** Over-index on serving and understanding
  them rather than chasing scale.

**Apply to:** early sales, hiring/firing, where to focus attention pre-scale.

## The VC lens as a decision test
A useful (conditional) gut check for venture-scale founders: *does this decision make the
company more or less fundable / more or less venture-scale?* Does it expand the market,
sharpen differentiation, accelerate growth, or strengthen the "why now"? If a decision
quietly shrinks the ceiling, name that — even if it's the safe choice.

But apply the caveat at the top: for a bootstrapper, "more fundable" is the wrong yardstick.
The right test there is "does this get me to durable profitability and independence?" Match
the yardstick to the game the founder is actually playing.
