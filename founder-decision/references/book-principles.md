# The Canon, Distilled

Actionable principles from the entrepreneurial canon — organized so you can apply the
*idea* to a live decision without lecturing. Cite the source only when it adds weight;
usually just use the principle.

## Zero to One — Peter Thiel
- **Aim for monopoly, not competition.** Competition erodes profit. Own a small market
  completely before expanding, rather than fighting for scraps in a big one.
- **The contrarian question:** "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"
  A great startup is built on a secret — something true that others haven't figured out.
- **Go from 0 to 1, not 1 to n.** Vertical progress (new things) beats horizontal copying.
  Ask: is this genuinely differentiated, or a slightly-better clone?
- **Start small and monopolize a niche**, then scale out. Dominating a tiny market is a
  feature, not a limitation.
- **Last-mover advantage.** Being first matters less than being the last to make a big
  improvement in a category and then holding it.

**Apply to:** differentiation, market choice, competitive strategy, "should we enter this
space?"

## The Hard Thing About Hard Things — Ben Horowitz
- **There are no easy answers to the hard things.** For genuine dilemmas, stop hunting for
  the good option — there often isn't one. Pick the least-bad and own it.
- **Wartime vs. peacetime.** In a crisis, normal consensus-building can be fatal; decisive,
  even unpopular calls are needed. Match your leadership mode to the situation.
- **Take care of people, product, profits — in that order,** but don't confuse being liked
  with leading. Hard calls (layoffs, firing a friend) are the job.
- **The struggle is normal.** Feeling like it's falling apart is not evidence you chose
  wrong; it's the standard texture of building something hard.

**Apply to:** firing, layoffs, survival calls, co-founder conflict, morale, "there's no
good option" moments.

## The Lean Startup — Eric Ries
- **Build–Measure–Learn.** Turn assumptions into cheap experiments. Before betting, ask:
  what's the smallest test that would tell me if I'm right?
- **Validated learning over vanity metrics.** Progress = learning what customers actually
  want, proven by their behavior, not by traffic or press.
- **Minimum viable product.** Ship the smallest thing that tests the core hypothesis. Don't
  build the cathedral before you know anyone wants to pray there.
- **Pivot or persevere** — decided by data from real experiments, not mood or fatigue.

**Apply to:** "should we build X?", launch scope, validation, pivot decisions, reducing
the cost of being wrong.

## Shoe Dog — Phil Knight
- **Cash flow is oxygen.** Nike nearly died repeatedly not from lack of demand but from
  growth outrunning cash. Growth you can't finance can kill you.
- **Persistence through relentless ambiguity.** The path is rarely clear; conviction and
  stamina through the fog are the job, not a detour from it.
- **Have a crazy idea and don't stop.** Belief in the mission carries you through the parts
  that look, from the outside, like failure.

**Apply to:** cash management, financing growth, when to push vs. pull back, endurance
during a rough stretch.

## Start-up Nation — Dan Senor & Saul Singer
- **Resourcefulness over resources.** Constraints breed ingenuity; scarcity is not an
  excuse. Israeli startups win by improvising, not by having more.
- **Chutzpah / productive audacity.** Challenge authority and assumptions; ask the bold
  question; don't defer just because someone's senior.
- **Tolerance for intelligent failure.** A failed venture done well isn't a stain — it's
  experience. Fear of failure is more dangerous than failure.

**Apply to:** operating under constraints, boldness vs. caution, cultural/attitude calls,
reframing "we don't have enough."

## Paul Graham / Y Combinator
- **Make something people want.** The whole game in one line.
- **Do things that don't scale.** Early on, manually recruit users, delight them one by one.
  Unscalable effort is how you find and earn the first real fans.
- **Talk to your users.** Almost every early answer is out there in conversation with the
  people you're building for.
- **Default alive vs. default dead.** Know whether, on current revenue growth and burn, you
  reach profitability before the money runs out — *before* you need to.
- **Be relentlessly resourceful.** The best predictor of a founder.

**Apply to:** early traction, PMF, user research, growth tactics, "are we actually alive?"

## Naval Ravikant
- **Play long-term games with long-term people.** Compounding trust and reputation beats
  any single win. Choose partners, investors, and customers you'll want to know in 20 years.
- **Specific knowledge + accountability + leverage.** Build the rare skill only you have,
  put your name on outcomes, then apply leverage (code, media, capital).
- **Earn with your mind, not your time.** Prefer decisions that build equity and leverage
  over ones that just trade hours.

**Apply to:** partnerships, who to work with, personal strategy, leverage, long-term vs.
short-term tradeoffs.

## The Innovator's Dilemma — Clayton Christensen
- **Disruption comes from below.** Incumbents ignore cheap, "inferior" entrants serving
  overlooked segments — until those entrants move upmarket and eat them. Being underestimated
  is an advantage.
- **Great companies fail by doing everything "right"** for their existing best customers,
  which blinds them to disruptive shifts.

**Apply to:** competing against incumbents, choosing a beachhead, whether an "inferior"
cheaper approach is actually the wedge.

## Crossing the Chasm — Geoffrey Moore
- **The chasm** between early adopters (who tolerate rough products) and the mainstream
  (who want a complete, proven solution) kills many startups. Don't mistake early-adopter
  love for mainstream demand.
- **Target a beachhead segment** and dominate it completely before broadening. A narrow,
  fully-served niche is the bridge across the chasm.

**Apply to:** go-to-market, segment focus, "why did early buzz stall?", scaling from first
fans to a real market.
