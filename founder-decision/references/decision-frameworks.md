## Two-way vs one-way doors (Bezos)
Classify by reversibility before anything else.
- **Two-way (reversible):** low cost to undo. Decide fast, delegate freely, treat as an
  experiment. Over-deliberating here is the actual mistake.
- **One-way (irreversible):** high or permanent cost to undo — equity, key hires, taking
  investment, public commitments, burning trust. Slow down, gather info, scrutinize.
The classic error is treating reversible calls as one-way (paralysis) and irreversible
calls as two-way (recklessness). Diagnose which you're facing first.

## Pre-mortem (Gary Klein)
Prospective hindsight. Imagine it's ~12 months later and the decision failed badly. Ask
"*why* did it fail?" and write the story. Because explaining a failure is easier than
predicting one, this surfaces risks that forward-looking optimism buries.
Steps: (1) assume failure as fact; (2) each plausible cause, listed concretely; (3) pick
the 2–3 most likely/severe; (4) ask whether they're survivable and whether the decision
can be adjusted to defuse them now. A failure mode the founder can't stomach is a stop sign.

## Expected value / decision tree
For decisions with quantifiable outcomes. For each option, enumerate outcomes, assign a
rough probability and a magnitude (value) to each, multiply and sum. But **do not stop at
raw EV** — check the shape of the downside. A positive-EV bet with a ruinous, unsurvivable
tail is still a bad bet for a founder who only gets to play once. Favor capped downside /
open upside (asymmetry).

## 10/10/10
Ask how you'll feel about this decision 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years from now. Cheap,
fast antidote to letting a momentary emotion (fear, flattery, frustration) drive a call
whose consequences play out over years.

## WRAP (Chip & Dan Heath, *Decisive*) — the four villains and their fixes
1. **Widen your options** — escape narrow "whether or not X" framing; find a third path.
   (If you're choosing between exactly two options, you probably haven't looked hard enough.)
2. **Reality-test your assumptions** — seek disconfirming evidence; run a small experiment;
   ask what would have to be true.
3. **Attain distance before deciding** — use 10/10/10, or ask what you'd advise a friend.
4. **Prepare to be wrong** — set a tripwire: a predefined signal that tells you to revisit
   or reverse. Decide *now* what evidence would change your mind.

## Disagree and commit
When a team can't reach consensus on a reversible call, someone decides and everyone commits
fully, even dissenters. Prevents decision paralysis and half-hearted execution. Reserve real
consensus-building for the irreversible, high-stakes calls where it's worth the time.

## Cheapest test first (de-risking)
Before committing resources to a bet, ask: what's the least expensive action that would
meaningfully reduce the biggest uncertainty? A landing page, five customer calls, a
concierge/manual version, a pre-sale. Buying information cheaply beats betting expensively.
This is the Lean Startup instinct as a reusable move.

## Reversible-decision speed rule
For a reversible decision, the cost of deliberation often exceeds the cost of being wrong.
Set a time box, decide, and use a tripwire to catch it if it goes bad. Speed is itself a
competitive advantage; a good decision now usually beats a perfect one next month.
