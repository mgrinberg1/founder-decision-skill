# Why Startups Actually Die

Use this to cross-check a decision: does it push the venture *toward* or *away from* one
of these graves? When a decision matches a known killer, name it plainly.

The top-level list draws on post-mortem analyses (e.g., CB Insights' recurring findings)
and hard-won operator experience. These are not rare edge cases — they're how most
startups end.

## The common causes of death

1. **No market need (the #1 killer).**
   Built something nobody actually wanted badly enough to pay for. The founder fell in
   love with a solution, not a problem. **Decision smell:** building/expanding before any
   evidence of real demand; "if we build it they'll come."

2. **Ran out of cash / couldn't raise.**
   The clock ran out. Often downstream of spending ahead of validation, or hiring/scaling
   before revenue justified it. **Decision smell:** committing fixed costs (hires, offices,
   long contracts) against uncertain revenue; assuming the next round is guaranteed.

3. **Wrong team.**
   Missing a key skill, co-founder conflict, wrong early hires, no one who can sell.
   **Decision smell:** hiring for pedigree over fit; avoiding a needed hard conversation;
   equity splits that will breed resentment.

4. **Out-competed.**
   A stronger or faster competitor took the market. Usually a symptom of no real
   differentiation or moat. **Decision smell:** entering a red ocean with a me-too product;
   ignoring an incumbent's structural advantage.

5. **Pricing / cost problems.**
   Priced too low to be sustainable, or a cost structure that never allows profitability.
   **Decision smell:** competing on price as the primary lever; unit economics that only
   work "at scale" with no path to get there.

6. **Product / timing problems.**
   Right idea, wrong time (too early, market not ready; or too late). Or a product that
   never reached "good enough." **Decision smell:** betting everything on a market that
   doesn't exist *yet* with no runway to wait for it.

7. **Lost focus / pivot gone wrong.**
   Chased too many things, or pivoted repeatedly without conviction until the core eroded.
   **Decision smell:** adding a second product before the first works; serial pivoting to
   escape discomfort rather than following evidence.

8. **Founder burnout / lost passion / disharmony.**
   The human system failed before the business did. **Decision smell:** decisions made from
   exhaustion or resentment; ignoring co-founder relationship debt.

## Recurring anti-patterns (the how, not the what)

- **Solution in search of a problem.** Starting from a cool technology and hunting for a
  use, rather than from a painful problem people already have.
- **Validating with the wrong people.** Friends, family, and polite strangers say
  "great idea." Only a payment (or a serious commitment) is real validation.
- **Confusing motion for progress.** Busy building, shipping, meeting — with no movement
  on the one metric that signals real demand.
- **Premature scaling.** Spending on growth (hiring, ads, infra) before product-market fit.
  The single most common way funded startups die.
- **Hiring to look real.** Adding headcount for the appearance of a "real company" before
  there's work that only a hire can do.
- **Avoiding the hard conversation.** Delaying a needed firing, co-founder reckoning, or
  honest talk with a customer — the cost compounds.
- **Fundraising as a goal.** Treating a raise as success rather than fuel. Money postpones
  the reckoning; it doesn't replace demand.
- **Feature factory.** Answering weak sales with more features instead of asking whether
  anyone wants the core.
- **Ignoring the default-dead math.** Not knowing your runway and growth rate well enough
  to say whether, on current trajectory, you live or die.
