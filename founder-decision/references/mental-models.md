# Mental Models for Founder Decisions

The transversal toolkit. Pull the 2–3 that cut to the heart of the decision — don't
spray all of them. Each entry: what it is, the question it forces, and the failure it
prevents.

## Reversibility (two-way vs one-way doors)
Most decisions are reversible; treat those as experiments and decide fast. Reserve
deliberation for the truly irreversible.
**Forces:** "If this is wrong, how expensive is it to undo?"
**Prevents:** Agonizing over cheap-to-reverse calls; rushing permanent ones.

## Sunk cost fallacy
Money, time, and effort already spent are gone regardless of what you choose next. Only
future costs and benefits should drive the decision.
**Forces:** "Would I start this today, knowing what I know now?"
**Prevents:** Throwing good money/time after bad because you're already "in."

## Opportunity cost
Every yes is a no to everything else you could do with that time, capital, and focus. The
real cost of a choice is the best alternative you forgo.
**Forces:** "What am I giving up the ability to do if I commit here?"
**Prevents:** Saying yes to good things that crowd out great ones. Founder focus is the
scarcest resource.

## Expected value & asymmetry
Weigh outcomes by their probability *and* magnitude. Above raw EV, favor **asymmetric**
bets: limited, known downside and large, open-ended upside (Taleb's barbell). Avoid the
inverse — small upside, catastrophic tail risk — no matter how likely the small win.
**Forces:** "What's the worst case if I'm wrong, and can I survive it? What's the ceiling
if I'm right?"
**Prevents:** Blowing up on a hidden tail risk; passing on cheap lottery tickets with huge
upside.

## First principles
Reason up from what is physically, economically, or logically true rather than by analogy
to how things are usually done. Especially powerful when the "standard way" is expensive
or when incumbents assume a constraint that isn't real.
**Forces:** "What do I actually know to be true here, ignoring convention?"
**Prevents:** Inheriting industry assumptions that don't apply to you.

## Inversion
Instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to guarantee failure — then avoid that. Often
easier and more actionable than forward planning.
**Forces:** "What would reliably make this a disaster?"
**Prevents:** Blind spots that a positive framing skips over. (This is the engine behind
the pre-mortem.)

## Second-order thinking
Ask "and then what?" Consequences have consequences. The first-order effect can be good
while the second- and third-order effects sink you (e.g., a discount that wins a customer
but trains your whole market to expect discounts).
**Forces:** "What happens *after* the immediate result — and after that?"
**Prevents:** Optimizing a local win that creates a bigger downstream problem.

## Circle of competence
Know the boundary of what you actually understand. Inside it, trust your judgment; outside
it, get help or stay out.
**Forces:** "Do I actually understand this domain, or am I guessing with confidence?"
**Prevents:** Overconfident calls in unfamiliar territory (new market, new tech, new
regulatory regime).

## Regret minimization
Project to the end of your life (Bezos) or a decade out and ask which choice you'd regret
less. Cuts through short-term fear and status anxiety on genuinely personal, high-stakes
calls (quitting a job, starting up, big pivots).
**Forces:** "At 80, which version of this will I wish I'd chosen?"
**Prevents:** Letting short-term fear override a long-term conviction.

## Base rates / outside view
Before trusting your inside story about why *this* time is different, ask what usually
happens to ventures/decisions like this one. The outside view is a humbling correction to
founder optimism.
**Forces:** "What's the success rate of this kind of move, historically?"
**Prevents:** Planning-fallacy optimism; ignoring how the reference class actually fares.

## Via negativa
Improvement often comes from removing, not adding — cutting a bad customer, killing a
feature, dropping a channel. Subtraction is underrated because it's less visible than
building.
**Forces:** "What should I *stop* doing?"
**Prevents:** Complexity creep; solving problems by piling on instead of removing the cause.

## Local vs global maximum
You can be climbing steadily (optimizing what you have) and still be on the wrong hill. To
reach a higher peak sometimes you must first go *down* — a painful pivot or reset.
**Forces:** "Am I improving toward a ceiling that's lower than another path entirely?"
**Prevents:** Endless incremental optimization of a fundamentally capped position.
